Job Market Paper: “Seller reputation with an Imperfect Review System” (Job Market Paper)

Abstract: I model the transactions that take place in an electronic marketplace where both adverse selection and moral hazard problems are present. A built-in review system is employed within the platform to ease the information problem and provide an incentive for the seller through reputation. Successful and unsuccessful transactions receive reviews with possibly distinct probabilities. These probabilities affect the type of Perfect Bayesian Equilibrium and the effort provided by the seller. I show that when the price is fixed, increasing the likelihood of a successful transaction being reviewed on the platform never decreases the seller's effort, with the possibility of a discrete jump. By contrast, increasing the likelihood of a failed transaction receiving a review may cause a discrete drop in the seller's effort. The seller's profit is non-monotonous in the probabilities of receiving reviews. Under endogenous price, the seller effort is continuous and non-monotonous in both parameters of the review system.

“A model of Competition between Offline firms and the Online Market” (Job Market Paper)

Abstract: In the recent decade, we witnessed a significant expansion of online commerce (e-commerce). Today, a consumer can find any product online from many other suppliers without geographical limitations in addition to the local supplier of the products. Hence, consumers who need a product have two options: They can go to the local market and choose the product that fits best to their interest upon observing available products or can search online until they believe they have found the appropriate product and purchase it. This option of the consumer brings in another dimension to the market. The sellers of two environments (local and online) with comparative advantages compete for the consumers' demand. Once in the brick-and-mortar seller's shop, the consumer can physically inspect the product and try it on if needed and find the best-fitting alternative according to her preferences. On the other hand, if the consumer decides to search for the product online, she has to visit the product's web page to get familiar with its features. Considering the vast number of online alternatives, a consumer who sequentially inspects the online products cannot ensure finding the best-fitting alternative. Instead, she has to decide if the product is good enough such that it is not worth searching for the next alternative. In the second chapter of the thesis, I aim to explore the short-term reaction (price) of the brick-and-mortar retailers who are competing against each other a la Hotelling to the competition against a perfectly competitive online market when the consumers are heterogeneous with respect to their distance to the shops and online search costs.

“Product Market Outcomes with Targeted and Random Advertising” (Job Market Paper)

Abstract: There is an ongoing debate around what is the best practice for personal data protection or data privacy. It is a highly multidisciplinary topic, and the debate touches on many vital issues. There are many aspects coming from the literature of business, law, ethics, and human rights. My main goal is to provide an economic perspective to the debate around personal data protection. The targeting advertisement is one of the major sectors where personal data is capitalized. Indeed, in almost all websites and social media, fine-tuned tailored ads are shown to the users based on their characteristics. Informative Advertising is argued to be informative for the consumers and generate a more competitive environment. On the other hand, targeted advertising can distort the consumers’ information inflow and hurt competition by generating markets where sellers have virtual market power by accessing the consumers who are willing to pay the highest price for their products and charge higher markups. Moreover, the traditional advertising expenditure can be wasteful because it does not have an efficient trade turnover, while targeted advertisement resolves the issue by reaching consumers with the highest likelihood of purchasing. In the digital advertising business, the market power is concentrated on the big tech companies with continuous access to vast consumer data. These intermediary advertisement providers have access to data from both sides of the product market, and the market power of the intermediary may enable the information flow control. Depending on how data flows from one side to another, the product market can have different outcomes. Therefore, my question is, does the targeting capability of Ad intermediaries affect the outcome of product markets without having an active role in the product market? Furthermore, does the market power in the Ad business can generate markets with higher markups and fewer socially beneficial trades? The objective of the third project is to find answers to these questions. For this purpose, I set up a theoretical model that reflects the characteristics of advertising mentioned above. I show that the targeted advertising improves total welfare created in the product market compared to random advertising, given that the marginal advertising costs are equal. The cost of the targeted advertising is passed to the consumers through the price but not random advertising; therefore, the product price is higher with targeted advertising. However, the sellers’ advertising expenditure is more efficient with targeting, which dominates the decrease in consumer welfare. The targeted advertising enables a market with a low-valued product while the market fails if only random advertising is available. If the market is functional with either type of advertising, consumers prefer random advertising since the price is higher with targeted ads than random ads, and fewer consumers purchase.
